# Visual Target Lock

This document is a non-negotiable guardrail for the supermarket game presentation.

## Source of truth

The product owner's uploaded final-effect image is the visual acceptance target. Every environment, character, fixture, prop, camera, lighting, and HUD decision must be compared against that image.

The target is not a loose mood reference. A playable screenshot that does not read as the same kind of scene is not visually complete.

## Locked composition

- Logical canvas: 1600 × 900 landscape.
- Fixed third-person camera with a slight downward angle.
- Employee and restock cart are the foreground visual subject.
- Produce department occupies the left side with dense, realistic merchandise.
- Staff/backroom entrance and work route create the centre depth axis.
- Beverage cooler occupies the right side and recedes naturally into the store.
- Floor, walls, ceiling, fixtures, characters, and props share one perspective and one lighting model.
- The scene must have clear foreground, middle ground, and background depth.

## Locked visual language

- Semi-realistic to realistic supermarket simulation.
- English-only production UI and store signage.
- Warm store lighting contrasted with cool refrigerator lighting.
- Natural contact shadows, fixture grounding, material highlights, and subtle floor reflection.
- High merchandise density; only the active target area should look understocked.
- One yellow target indicator at a time.
- Minimal HUD placed inside safe areas without covering store signs or the active fixture.

## Forbidden drift

The project must not drift into any of the following:

- top-down puzzle presentation;
- flat geometric placeholder art presented as final;
- mixed cartoon, vector, photographic, and realistic styles in the same production scene;
- front-facing character portrait as the gameplay camera;
- oversized fixture images pasted over the environment;
- day-specific presentation architecture or asset ownership;
- Chinese text baked into production art;
- multiple simultaneous arrows, outlines, or tutorial indicators;
- HUD panels covering the character, task target, or department signs;
- free-camera or full-3D scope expansion before the fixed-camera experience is approved.

## Review order for every screenshot

1. Does the scene immediately read as a supermarket work simulator?
2. Is the employee/cart the foreground subject?
3. Are produce, backroom, and beverage zones in the locked left/centre/right arrangement?
4. Do all visible elements share perspective, scale, light direction, and realism level?
5. Is the current task target obvious without clutter?
6. Does the HUD remain readable without becoming the main visual subject?
7. Is the result materially closer to the approved target than the previous screenshot?

A change that improves code quality but moves the screenshot away from these rules must be revised before promotion.
